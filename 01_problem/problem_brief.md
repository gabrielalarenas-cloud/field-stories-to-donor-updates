# Problem Brief
## The Field-to-Donor Storytelling Gap

## Executive summary

Many NGOs rely on field teams to provide the raw material that helps donors understand the impact of their support: short videos, photos, testimonials, program updates, and local context.

This content is important because donor trust depends on timely, credible, human-centered communication.

But the current process often places too much burden on field teams and creates delays for HQ communications teams. The result is a storytelling gap: the people closest to the work have the least time and support to communicate it, while the people responsible for donor engagement are often farthest from the field context.

This proof of concept explores how ElevenLabs could support a responsible workflow for turning consent-based field updates into multilingual donor communications.

## The core business problem

This is not only a content problem. It is a partnership, operations, and donor trust problem.

North American and European NGOs often work through field teams across Africa, LATAM, and Asia. These local teams are closest to the programs, communities, and beneficiaries. They understand the context and can explain what is happening in real time.

However, they are usually responsible for program delivery, not donor storytelling.

When HQ needs donor updates, fundraising materials, campaign assets, or impact reports, field teams are often asked to provide photos, videos, voice notes, written updates, beneficiary quotes, local context, progress data, and translation support.

These requests are often urgent, fragmented, and added on top of existing field responsibilities.

## What breaks in the current process

### 1. Field teams are overburdened

Field staff are hired to deliver programs, support communities, coordinate local partners, and solve operational problems. Asking them to also create donor-ready communications can create extra pressure and resentment.

### 2. Updates lose freshness

By the time photos, videos, and stories move from the field to HQ, are translated, reviewed, edited, and repackaged, the moment may no longer feel current.

### 3. Language barriers slow everything down

Field teams may share updates in Spanish, French, Swahili, Arabic, Hindi, or other local languages. HQ teams and donors may need English, Spanish, French, or other donor-facing languages. Translation delays can reduce both speed and nuance.

### 4. Storytelling quality is inconsistent

Some field updates are powerful but raw. Others are operationally useful but not donor-ready. Without a repeatable workflow, quality depends too much on individual staff capacity.

### 5. Emotional context can be lost

The goal is not to manufacture emotion. The goal is to preserve the human context of the work without distorting it.

When too many people edit or translate a story, the final version can become polished but less authentic. At the same time, overly emotional storytelling can risk becoming extractive if not handled carefully.

### 6. Consent and safeguarding are hard to track

If stories, images, or voice recordings move through informal channels, it becomes harder to confirm whether consent was collected, whether the story is safe to share, and whether the person represented understands how the material will be used.

## Why this matters

A better system could help NGOs reduce field team workload, improve donor update frequency, communicate across languages faster, preserve local context, improve donor trust, maintain stronger consent and safeguarding controls, and create reusable workflows across programs and countries.

## Design question

How might an NGO use AI voice and dubbing tools to transform raw, consent-based field updates into timely, multilingual donor communications without replacing human judgment, weakening consent, or exploiting emotional storytelling?

## Proposed solution direction

Use ElevenLabs as part of a human-in-the-loop workflow where:

1. Field staff submit short raw updates in their own language
2. HQ reviews the update for consent, safety, accuracy, and relevance
3. The update is transcribed and translated
4. A donor-ready script is drafted and reviewed
5. ElevenLabs generates multilingual narration or dubbing
6. HQ publishes the final update with appropriate disclosure and safeguards
7. The NGO tracks field staff effort, donor engagement, and partner adoption

## What this proof of concept demonstrates

This repository does not attempt to build a production product.

It demonstrates the operating logic behind a possible deployment: the user problem, NGO partner scenario, creative blueprint, content workflow, ElevenLabs use case, consent and safeguarding controls, rollout model, success metrics, and global partnership scaling potential.
